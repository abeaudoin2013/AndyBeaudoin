I Was Born in (and Left) the USA

In October I said I was going to go back to France.

In January I applied.

In February, March, April and May I waited until the news came through.

In June, a weekend in Chicago: visa, birth certificate, banking, house-hunting, working between Midland and Lansing. Waiting.

In August I handed over the lease. Moved out of the room. Moved from couch to floor to couch. Work, work, work. Wait, wait, wait, and hear people say, “You’re going to find some French girl."

September came, but moved slowly; in complete exhaustion of my patience I found myself pushing through the next few days in the States with a one-way ticket, one week of accommodation with a friend of an ex-professor, packing and playing Midnight in Paris on repeat.

At the airport terminal, we, the other college student, the businessman, the L.A. mother and myself, discovered that American Airlines does not keep track of their flights very well.

Ran to the connecting flight in O'Hare. Took a sleepless flight to Paris where the mother from Ghana and I pointed out the faux pas of AA: "I’m flying United next time,” she said.

Airport, customs, passport/visa stamped, 50 pounds of luggage; then in RER B a man with a disintegrating violin and his daughter (on the tambourine) played a gypsy tune and for the first time since the last time I was in France I felt like I was in France for the first time again–so, yeah, I gave them one Euro.

Took RER D with specific instructions not to get off at the first stop. Got off at the first stop because we could not stop at the second stop. Where to go? “Monsieur! Prennez le bus.” So I took the bus.

Got off in Saint-Denis where a flood of foreigners traversed the streets in completely random but straight lines, always nearing a collision but never quite colliding, always passing through but not passively.

Down the street to the theater to the convent to the boulangerie. “The very tall, pink building,” said the e-mail.

I buzzed the buzzer on the name of my one-week-long host. It buzzed back.

“Hello?"

No answer.

Opened the door and find the mailbox with the name and the number, 27. Perfect. I got into the elevator to find the second floor–the buttons are labeled: 3, 5, 7, 9, etc. Got out of the elevator. Went back outside, rebuzzed, it rebuzzed back.

"Hello?”

No answer.

“Merde."

I went back inside and to my relief a head poked itself out of the doorway of the stairwell and said: "Andy?”

Thank the lord. Here’s your room, here’s the shower, here are my good friends who by the way are college professors. Turned my French on. Mostly listened.

I am staying with someone named Céline. Due to my exhaustion, the names of her friends, a woman and her boyfriend, escaped me. They talked and I listened: the things we don’t know about our digestive system; my favorite rugby teams are the ones who lose; in France we call herbal tea pisse mémé, granny’s piss; children’s books are so full of kindness. Things like that.

The man had a huge beard and while we were eating hamburgers (that's am-buh-gur), food fell in it so Céline read out loud a book where this happens to a homeless man. There were pictures.

Then she showed me around Saint-Denis (a suburb just north of Paris): over 160 nationalities live here. You’re not a minority because everyone in Saint-Denis is.

And now I’m sitting at a small table in small flat in a suburb just north of Paris, edges from the beginning of October and a new job and I’m wondering what it all was that got me here.

My second journey with France has started and we’ve got a long way to go.
